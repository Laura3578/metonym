What you face

Four kinds of inquiry, one underlying question.

Litigation discovery. If your AI system has interacted with a user who later self-harmed, plaintiff's counsel will request every document touching your evaluation of how the system handles such users. "We had safety filters" is not responsive. "We commissioned independent clinical evaluation by a licensed psychologist, with citable methodology, dated [before the incident]" is.

Regulatory inquiry. FTC 6(b) orders, state-AG civil investigative demands, EU AI Act conformity assessment requests, MDR clinical-evidence requirements. All ask, in different language, the same question: what is your documented evaluation of how this system handles consequential conversations?

Insurance and D&O carrier review. AI-specific E&O and D&O underwriting now asks about clinical evaluation specifically. The carrier that asks "have you been clinically evaluated?" is not asking whether your engineering team thinks the system is safe. They're asking for an external instrument.

Board and audit-committee reporting. Boards increasingly ask AI-deploying companies a version of: "What is our process for making sure this thing doesn't end us?" The defensible answer is documented, dated, third-party clinical evaluation — referenced in the minutes.

All four are versions of the same question: what evidence do you have on file?

The regulatory and litigation situation specific to you

The doctrinal and enforcement landscape that reshapes your evidentiary posture.

  • Garcia v. Character Technologies (M.D. Fla., May 2025). "Product, not speech" ruling. First Amendment defense rejected. Product-liability and FDUTPA claims proceeding. AI safety failures are now product-liability exposures. Full coverage on the regulation page →
  • Pennsylvania v. Character Technologies (filed May 1, 2026). First US state-AG action alleging unlicensed practice of medicine by an AI chatbot. Testing whether AI behavior can constitute unlicensed practice without the company claiming to practice.
  • FTC 6(b) inquiry (Sept 11, 2025). Active, naming seven AI companies. Section 5 deceptive-practices framework. Worth understanding regardless of whether your company was named.
  • Texas AG investigation (Aug 18, 2025). Civil Investigative Demands issued for deceptive mental-health marketing.
  • State AI mental-health laws. IL HB 1806, UT HB 452, CA SB 243, NY AI Companion Law — each creates a different evidentiary standard your team will be asked to meet.
  • EU AI Act high-risk obligations. Article 9 (risk management), Article 10 (data governance), Article 11 (technical documentation), Article 14 (human oversight), Article 15 (accuracy/robustness/cybersecurity), Article 72 (post-market monitoring) — most effective Aug 2, 2026.
  • EU MDR 2017/745 and IVDR 2017/746. Software-as-a-Medical-Device clinical evaluation expectations (Annex XIV/XV) apply to MH-positioned AI in the EU market.

The Compliance page → maps these into the four operational compliance domains — disclosure, clinical oversight, data privacy, post-market monitoring — and shows where a Metonym evaluation deliverable plugs in.

Which engagement model fits

The right Metonym engagement depends on whether you're prospective or reactive.

Prospective: documented clinical evaluation on file. If your company is in market and has not yet been the subject of a regulatory letter, the right move is to commission documented clinical evaluation now — before anyone asks. A Service 01 Clinical Risk Review (2–4 weeks, fixed scope) produces a written deliverable, dated, with citable methodology. Filed with the GC's office; referenced in board minutes; cited in your insurance application. The evidence exists before the inquiry does.

Prospective with iteration: ongoing safety governance evidence. If your product changes frequently — new model versions, new safety rules, new features that affect MH-adjacent conversation — a Service 03 Safety Design & Co-Design Advisory retainer keeps the evidence current and produces documentation suitable for regulatory submissions and ongoing post-market surveillance under EU AI Act Article 72 or analogous frameworks.

Reactive: incident, inquiry, or pre-litigation. If something has gone wrong, a regulator has contacted you, or your outside counsel is preparing for a likely plaintiff's letter, Service 04 Expert Review & Incident Analysis is the engagement model. Operates under privilege coordinated with your retained counsel. Forensic-grade analysis suitable for evidentiary use. Dr. Walsh's underlying forensic practice — wrongful-death and suicide-related evaluation in civil and employment matters — directly informs this work.

Pre-disclosure / pre-press: launch-readiness or scale-up. If you're approaching a launch, a press cycle, or M&A diligence, a Service 02 Failure-Mode & Red Team engagement (4–8 weeks) surfaces the failure patterns most likely to produce the kind of incident a journalist or plaintiff's expert would write about.

Where you are in your company's lifecycle

Stage shapes the scope. The evidentiary need doesn't change.

In-house counsel at a pre-launch / pre-revenue company. Cost-calibrated Service 01 engagement before launch. Produces the documented clinical evaluation that future-you wants on file when the company is no longer pre-launch and somebody is asking what you did.

GC / Risk officer at a funded / growth-stage company. Service 01 or Service 02 at the next major version release, plus consideration of Service 03 retainer as the company scales toward the user-base threshold where incidents become statistically likely.

GC / CRO at an established or public company. Continuous Service 03 plus Service 04 standby. Evidence documented quarterly, referenced in 10-K AI risk disclosures where applicable, and integrated into the company's broader AI risk governance program.

Outside counsel managing AI risk for client companies. Service 04 retainer relationship with Dr. Walsh's forensic practice (see About) is available for matters under privilege; coordination with client GC and in-house compliance.

What's at stake

The single most expensive evaluation is the one you didn't do.

Prospective clinical evaluation is meaningfully cheaper than retrospective clinical evaluation under discovery pressure. Documented evaluation produced before an incident becomes evidence of reasonable diligence; documented evaluation produced after looks like a defensive posture. The doctrinal and enforcement landscape since Garcia has reset what "reasonable diligence" looks like for AI deployers in the mental-health-adjacent zone. Boards know it. Insurers know it. Plaintiff's bar knows it.
