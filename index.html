The Problem

Generic AI safety catches the wrong thing.

Most AI safety tooling for mental health was built by engineers, validated by engineers, and audited by engineers. It catches the obvious phrases — explicit ideation, named methods, profanity — and reports a passing score.

That is not the same as clinical safety. The most consequential failures of AI mental health systems are false negatives — moments where escalating risk was present, the system did not recognize it, and the conversation continued as if nothing had changed. These are the moments that produce wrongful-death lawsuits, regulatory inquiries, and front-page stories.

The clinically meaningful signals AI systems most often miss — decision-state transitions, collapse of perceived options, temporal narrowing, the calm that follows a settled plan — don't surface in the vocabulary that content moderators were trained on. They require clinical instruments.

The most consequential of these is the decision-state transition — the moment a conversation shifts from passive distress to active planning. Clinically, it's the single highest-risk shift in a developing crisis. Linguistically, it can occur without a single trigger word. A well-trained clinician notices it instinctively. Surface-feature filters do not.

Signals filters miss

The clinical moments AI mental-health systems get wrong most often.

Filters trained on trigger vocabulary catch the obvious phrases. They are blind to state-transition signals — the clinical moments where risk escalates fastest and conventional safety tooling produces a passing score on a failing conversation.

Passive ideation → active planning

The shift from "I wish I weren't here" to method-adjacent language. Often arrives without trigger vocabulary; filters trained on words like "kill" or "suicide" miss it entirely.

The post-decision calm

A sudden affective decrease after a plan has formed. Reads as "the user is feeling better." Clinically the opposite — and one of the highest-acuity moments in real-world risk.

Collapse of perceived options

When a person's solution space narrows to one. Language often remains composed; the underlying decision has already been made. Surface-feature analysis does not see this.

Temporal narrowing

Future-thinking constricts. Plans, references to next week, mentions of upcoming events drop out of the conversation. Rarely surfaces in vocabulary content moderators were trained on.
