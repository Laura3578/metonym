What Metonym is — and isn't.

Metonym is an independent clinical evaluation firm. We assess how AI systems handle conversations with users in acute psychological distress, using a patent-pending clinical methodology developed by a licensed clinical psychologist with 20+ years of practice.

Metonym does not build chatbots, fine-tune models, sell content moderation tools, or provide therapy to end users. We evaluate systems; we do not treat people. The deliverable a client receives is the kind of independent clinical assessment regulators, ethics boards, and litigation discovery increasingly expect.

How is this different from AI red-teaming or safety audits?

Conventional AI red-teaming finds adversarial inputs and policy violations. Conventional safety audits measure response to flagged trigger words. Both are useful. Neither evaluates whether the AI clinically recognizes the signals an experienced therapist would notice — temporal narrowing, collapse of perceived options, the calm that follows a settled plan, decision-state transitions.

Metonym evaluates the signals filters were never trained on. Our preprint research found 57% of clinically meaningful moments received responses scored as inadequately recognized across 31+ models — most of those moments would have passed a standard content-moderation audit cleanly. What does an engagement cost?

Engagement cost is set during the scoping call after we understand the system, the volume of scenarios, and the depth of analysis required. Engagements range from fixed-scope Clinical Risk Reviews (typically 25–50 interactions, multi-week turnaround) to multi-month Safety Design & Co-Design Advisory retainers and ongoing Expert Review support.

We do not publish list pricing because the appropriate engagement model depends on what you're trying to learn, which compliance regime you're navigating, and how much of your system needs evaluating. A 30-minute scoping call clarifies that in advance of any quote.

How long does a typical engagement take?

Indicative ranges — confirmed in scoping:

  • Clinical Risk Review: 2–4 weeks from kickoff to written report.
  • Failure-Mode & Red Team: 4–8 weeks depending on scenario breadth.
  • Safety Design & Co-Design Advisory: Ongoing retainer; minimum 3-month engagement.
  • Expert Review & Incident Analysis: Calibrated to inquiry or litigation timeline; rush engagements available.

Who owns the IP from an engagement?

The client owns the engagement deliverable (report, findings, recommendations) and may use it internally, share it with regulators, and produce it in litigation discovery as needed.

Metonym retains all underlying IP in the methodology itself — the Salient Distress Model (openly published, CC-BY-NC 4.0 via Zenodo), the Mechanical Severity Score scoring procedure (US Provisional Patent Application No. 64/059,837), and the evaluation pipeline. Clients receive findings produced by the methodology; they do not receive a license to operate the methodology themselves.

Is the engagement confidential?

Yes. Engagements operate under written confidentiality agreements that cover system internals, transcripts, findings, and the existence of the engagement itself if the client prefers. Forensic-grade analysis (Service 04) operates under appropriate privilege protections coordinated with client counsel.

Metonym never publishes case studies, uses client logos, or references client engagements publicly without explicit written consent. Aggregated, fully de-identified evaluation patterns may inform Metonym's published research — but only with consent and only in a form that cannot be traced back to any specific client or system.

Does Metonym evaluation satisfy specific regulations?

Metonym deliverables produce evaluation evidence. Whether that evidence satisfies a specific regulatory regime depends on the regime, the deployment context, and the client's broader compliance posture. We are not a law firm and we do not certify compliance.

That said, Metonym evaluation is designed to map directly to expectations that recurring regulatory frameworks set out — including:

  • FDA Clinical Decision Support Final Guidance (Sept 2022) and AI/ML Software-as-a-Medical-Device pre-market expectations
  • EU AI Act Annex III high-risk system obligations (Articles 9, 10, 11, 14, 15, 72)
  • State AI mental-health laws including IL HB 1806, UT HB 452, CA SB 243, and the NY AI Companion Models Law
  • MDR 2017/745 clinical evaluation expectations (Annex XIV/XV) for SaMD

Why a clinician instead of an engineering safety team?

Engineering safety teams are necessary. They are not sufficient. The signals that produce wrongful-death lawsuits, regulatory inquiries, and front-page stories are clinical signals — not policy-violation signals. They require clinical training to notice and clinical instruments to score reproducibly.

A two-decade clinical psychologist with forensic experience in wrongful-death matters reads conversational risk the way an experienced radiologist reads imaging. That kind of judgment cannot be retrofitted onto an engineering audit. It has to be in the framework from the start.

Can you evaluate our system without us handing over the model?

Yes. Most Metonym engagements operate on the input/output surface — we send carefully constructed scenarios into the system and evaluate what comes back, in the conditions your end users would experience. We do not need model weights, training data, or proprietary fine-tuning material.

For Safety Design & Co-Design Advisory engagements (Service 03), deeper access to system internals — guardrail logic, response chains, escalation rules — accelerates the work, but is negotiated in scoping with full IP protection.

Is the methodology proprietary, open, or both?

Both. The Salient Distress Model (SDM) — the clinical framework defining which signals an evaluator should look for — is openly published in the Zenodo methodology preprint (DOI 10.5281/zenodo.20147000) under CC-BY-NC 4.0. Anyone can cite it, teach from it, and use it for non-commercial purposes.

The Mechanical Severity Score (MSS) — the structured scoring procedure that turns clinical judgment into reproducible measurement — is patent-pending (US Provisional Application No. 64/059,837, filed 2026-05-07). It is the operational layer that lets Metonym evaluate the same system the same way across model versions, deployments, and time periods. Preprint is not peer-reviewed; journal submission to follow.

How do you handle bias and limitations in clinical evaluation?

Honestly. No clinical instrument is bias-free, and Metonym deliverables include explicit documentation of methodology limitations, sample-construction choices, and known evaluation blind spots. The methodology preprint discusses these in detail.

What Metonym does not do is overclaim. We do not assert "clinical certainty" because clinical certainty does not exist in suicide-risk detection. We assert clinically defensible, reproducible, replicable evaluation — and we document where the evaluation ends.
