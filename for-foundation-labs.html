For Foundation Model Labs

Independent clinical evaluation built for the way your safety teams actually work.

Your internal red team finds adversarial inputs and policy violations. Your alignment team trains models to refuse the obvious. Neither is the same as clinical evaluation of how your models recognize escalating suicide risk in real conversations. That gap is what Metonym was built to close — with a patent-pending methodology developed by a licensed clinical psychologist.

Who this page is for

This page is for Trust & Safety, Alignment, Safety Research, Responsible Scaling, and policy/compliance leaders at frontier AI labs — the organizations building general-purpose foundation models that millions of users converse with every day, on a wide range of topics, including (whether the product is marketed for it or not) psychological distress and suicide-adjacent conversation.

If you've ever had your evaluation team report a passing safety score on a scenario that, on closer reading, missed something a clinical psychologist would have caught — this page is for you.

What you face

Three pressures converging on foundation labs.

Scale exposes the long tail. A general-purpose model with hundreds of millions of users will see crisis-adjacent conversation at a daily volume that even your largest internal red team can't sample. Evaluation that's adequate at a thousand users is not adequate at a hundred million.

Capability and safety are increasingly hard to separate. The same RLHF and post-training process that improves helpfulness also reshapes how the model talks to users in distress. Each model version is, effectively, a different evaluation subject. Internal evaluation cadences built for capability benchmarks don't naturally cover the clinical signals where the model's behavior changes most consequentially.

"We have filters" is no longer the answer. The Sept 2025 FTC 6(b) inquiry into seven AI chatbot companies — including Alphabet, OpenAI, and others operating foundation models — explicitly named safety assessments for minors and crisis-adjacent users as in scope. Regulators are asking for evaluation evidence, not safety architecture diagrams. Your General Counsel will want documented independent clinical evaluation on file before that letter arrives, not after.

The regulatory situation specific to you

Foundation labs sit upstream of every consumer AI regulation now in force.

You don't directly fall under most state AI mental-health laws — those target deployers and consumer-facing products. But your downstream customers do, and your model's behavior is what gets evaluated when they're called to account. The regulatory weight rolls upstream.

  • FTC §5 inquiry (active). Orders issued Sept 11, 2025 to Alphabet, Character Technologies, Instagram, Meta, OpenAI, Snap, and xAI. Safety assessments and crisis-handling evidence requested directly from foundation-model operators. See regulation page →
  • EU AI Act Article 5(1)(f). Emotion-recognition prohibition in force since Feb 2, 2025, with a "medical or safety" exception. Foundation models deployed in workplace and education contexts must walk through that exception — and the exception requires clinical evidence, not engineering documentation.
  • EU AI Act high-risk obligations. Most provisions applying from Aug 2026 — including risk management (Article 9), data governance (Article 10), human oversight (Article 14), and post-market monitoring (Article 72). Foundation labs supplying high-risk downstream deployers will be asked to provide the underlying evaluation evidence.
  • Garcia v. Character Technologies precedent. May 2025 ruling that the chatbot is a product, not protected speech. Product-liability claims now survive First Amendment defense — and foundation models supplied to downstream products are not insulated from the product-liability theory of the underlying model layer.

Which engagement model fits

Two engagement patterns work for foundation labs.

Ongoing — Safety Design & Co-Design Advisory (Service 03). The right model for foundation labs running continuous safety evaluation as part of the RLHF and post-training cycle. Dr. Walsh embedded with your safety team on a quarterly or monthly cadence, reviewing model behavior changes across releases, calibrating response strategies, surfacing failure patterns specific to the SDM-defined high-risk states. Documentation produced is suitable for regulatory submissions and post-market surveillance evidence.

Periodic — Failure-Mode & Red Team (Service 02). Pre-release stress testing of a specific model version against a calibrated scenario set. 4–8 weeks, structured scenario set, multi-version comparison (current vs. proposed release vs. baseline), worked examples of high-risk misses with clinical explanation, prioritized remediation recommendations. Done before each major model release, or quarterly for slower release cadences.

Most foundation-lab engagements combine the two: a Service 03 retainer for continuous review, with Service 02 stress-test cycles around major releases.

Where you are in your scaling curve

Stage matters less than cadence here.

Pre-launch foundation labs are rare — most labs are scaling actively before public release. If you're pre-scale (training a frontier model that hasn't been released yet), the highest-leverage move is to lock the evaluation cadence before the model ships, not after. A single Service 02 cycle on the last pre-release checkpoint gives you launch-readiness evidence on file and establishes the baseline for the Service 03 ongoing review.

Funded / growth-stage labs typically have internal safety capacity but lack independent clinical perspective. Service 03 on a quarterly cadence fills that gap. The "independent" is doing important work — your downstream customers, your investors, and your regulators all weigh external evaluation evidence differently from internal evaluation evidence, even when the methodology is identical.

Established labs are the typical buyer profile. Quarterly Service 03 retainer with stepped-up Service 02 evaluation around major releases. Specific evaluation scope and frequency negotiated to match your release cadence and the size of your safety team.

What's at stake

The first foundation-lab AI mental-health front-page story will reshape the market.

Garcia v. Character Technologies established that AI chatbots can be sued as products, not protected as speech. Pennsylvania v. Character Technologies extended that to unlicensed practice of medicine. The next high-profile case will almost certainly involve a foundation model rather than a wrapper — and the central evidentiary question will be "what did you do to evaluate how your model handles users in distress?" The company that can produce documented independent clinical evaluation on file, dated before the incident, with citable methodology, will be the company still operating after.

The cost of independent clinical evaluation, prospectively, is a small fraction of the cost of producing it retrospectively under litigation discovery. The most expensive evaluation is the one you didn't do.
