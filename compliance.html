Compliance

The operational map for AI mental health tools.

Consumer-facing health and mental health chatbots sit at the intersection of FDA digital health policy, state mental health chatbot statutes, federal consumer-protection authority, and emerging professional-licensure law. This page is the practical compliance map — what to disclose, when to involve a clinician, what data you can and cannot use, and what to monitor after launch. It is not legal advice.

Who this is for

Product, clinical, legal, privacy, and safety teams shipping AI mental health features.

The checklist below is a requirements map for product risk triage, launch readiness, vendor diligence, and post-launch governance. It synthesizes the operational obligations imposed (explicitly or implicitly) by three concrete frameworks:

  • FDA Clinical Decision Support Software guidance — what software functions are device-regulated vs. excluded under Section 520(o)(1)(E) of the FD&C Act.
  • Utah HB 452 — the most operationally specific state statute for AI mental health chatbots; the policy framework it requires is a useful baseline regardless of jurisdiction.
  • Proposed CHATBOT Act (H.R. 7985) — would prohibit false claims of professional licensure or human verification.

For the underlying laws and litigation, see Regulation.

The four compliance domains

Disclosure · Clinical oversight · Data privacy · Post-market monitoring.

Any responsible AI mental health deployment should have an explicit answer in each of these domains, documented in a written policy.

01 · Disclosure mandates

The user knows they are talking to AI.

  • AI-not-human disclosure before first access, after extended inactivity, and whenever the user asks (per Utah HB 452 — applies regardless of jurisdiction as best practice).
  • No marketing or in-product output claiming licensed-professional status, clinical credentials, or human verification unless those facts are true and documented.
  • Explicit limitations and safe-use instructions: intended purpose, capabilities, what the tool is not for, when to seek human or emergency help.
  • Crisis limitations disclosed in-product, especially for mental health use cases.
  • Advertising disclosures — sponsored content clearly marked; user input not used to target ads.
Statutes Utah HB 452 (specific timing rules), CA SB 243 (minors), NY AI Companion Law, proposed CHATBOT Act (federal).

02 · Clinical oversight

A licensed professional is in the loop where it matters.

  • Licensed mental health therapist involvement in development and review for tools that engage in therapist-like conversations (Illinois HB 1806 makes this a legal requirement, not a courtesy).
  • AI is not making independent therapeutic decisions, directly providing therapy, or generating treatment plans without licensed-professional review (IL).
  • Human-in-the-loop review where claimed — and only where it is operationally real. See the HITL controls section below.
  • Risk escalation protocols for self-harm, harm to others, medical emergencies, psychosis, intoxication, and acute physical-harm signals.
  • Real-time response capability for acute physical-harm risk per Utah's affirmative-defense policy framework.
Statutes Illinois HB 1806 (strictest), Utah HB 452 affirmative-defense policy, FDA Non-Device CDS criteria.

03 · Data privacy

Mental-health-related data is not sold, shared, or used to target.

  • No sale or sharing of individually identifiable health information or user input, except under defined statutory pathways (Utah HB 452).
  • No use of user input to determine ad targeting, ad content, or ad customization — except for advertising the chatbot itself (Utah).
  • HIPAA-style privacy and security compliance for the product's policy framework, even if the supplier is not technically a covered entity (Utah affirmative defense).
  • Maintain a full data map: inputs, outputs, logs, model prompts, retrieval context, embeddings, training/fine-tuning use, analytics, service providers, retention periods.
  • Service-provider controls: contracts, access controls, deletion/return obligations, business-associate-style terms where required.
Statutes Utah HB 452, HIPAA, FTC Act §5, state health-privacy laws, GDPR for EU users.

04 · Post-market monitoring

You can prove the system is still working safely.

  • Pre-release testing — clinical accuracy, hallucinations, harmful advice, crisis responses, privacy leaks, discriminatory outputs, prompt injection, jailbreaks (Utah affirmative-defense framework).
  • Ongoing testing on a defined cadence and after material changes.
  • User mechanism to report harmful interactions, with documented review and response process.
  • Regular objective reviews of safety, accuracy, efficacy, fairness, and harmful-interaction patterns — internal or external audits where appropriate.
  • Documentation maintained: model cards, release notes, prompt/system policy changes, evaluation results, incident reports, reviewer logs, harm reports, audit findings.
Statutes Utah HB 452 (affirmative-defense documentation), FDA digital health policy, EU AI Act high-risk monitoring.

FDA Non-Device CDS — the four criteria

Are you regulated as a medical device, or excluded?

Under Section 520(o)(1)(E) of the FD&C Act, certain software is excluded from the device definition. To qualify, all four criteria must be met. Patient-facing AI mental health tools generally do not qualify.

  • Criterion 1. The software does not acquire, process, or analyze medical images, in vitro diagnostic signals, or signals from signal-acquisition systems.
  • Criterion 2. The software is intended to display, analyze, or print medical information about a patient or other medical information.
  • Criterion 3. The software is intended to support or provide recommendations to a health care professional about prevention, diagnosis, or treatment of a disease or condition — not directly to a patient or caregiver.
  • Criterion 4. The software is intended to enable the HCP to independently review the basis for the recommendations and not rely primarily on the software output for the clinical decision.

Consumer-facing AI mental health chatbots fail Criterion 3 (they speak directly to users) and often Criterion 4 (the rationale is opaque). FDA's position is that software functions meeting the device definition remain subject to digital health policy, including functions intended for patients or caregivers.

Sources: FDA CDS guidance page · Full FDA CDS guidance (PDF)

HITL — what "clinically reviewed" actually requires

Human-in-the-loop review is not a label. It is an operational discipline.

A "clinician verified" claim should match what your workflow actually delivers. Six controls separate meaningful HITL from rubber-stamping:

  • Qualified reviewer. The reviewer holds a license whose scope matches the recommendation domain. Documented licensure, jurisdiction, and scope mapping.
  • Independent basis review. The reviewer is shown the patient-specific facts used, source materials, rationale, uncertainty, limitations, and validation context — enough to evaluate and reject the recommendation.
  • Prospective review where needed. Patient-specific diagnosis, treatment, medication, triage, or crisis recommendations require review before the user sees them — not after.
  • Anti-rubber-stamping controls. Measure review time, override rate, edit rate, reviewer-agreement patterns, repeated failures. High agreement alone is not evidence of safe review.
  • Claim substantiation. Public claims align with actual workflow. "Clinician verified" should not be used for unreviewed, retrospective, sample-reviewed, or escalation-only outputs.
  • Change control. HITL adequacy is reassessed after model, prompt, retrieval, UI, clinical-content, or escalation-protocol changes.

Launch-gate checklist

What should be true before you ship.

Product and claims

  • Intended-use statement — defined: general wellness, administrative, HCP-facing CDS, consumer health info, mental health support, therapy-like support, diagnosis support, or crisis support.
  • Claim inventory — website copy, app-store listings, onboarding, in-chat system messages, ads, emails, and model outputs reviewed for therapy/diagnosis/treatment/licensure/credentialing/human-review claims.
  • User-role gating — if relying on FDA Non-Device CDS, intended use and workflow restricted to HCP decision support; consumers cannot receive unreviewed patient-specific recommendations as final advice.

Clinical governance

  • Clinical governance committee — product, clinical, safety, privacy, and legal ownership identified for launch approval and post-launch changes.
  • Licensed therapist involvement documented in development and review (Utah HB 452 requirement for affirmative-defense readiness).
  • Risk escalation workflows defined for self-harm, harm to others, medical emergencies, abuse, medication issues, severe mental illness.

Privacy and data

  • Data map documented — inputs, outputs, logs, prompts, retrieval context, embeddings, training use, analytics, service providers, retention.
  • Utah HB 452 data restrictions applied for Utah users (no selling/sharing health data or user input outside permitted pathways; no targeted advertising based on user input).
  • Service-provider controls in place — contracts, access controls, deletion obligations, HIPAA-style business associate terms where required.

Monitoring and incident response

  • Pre-release testing complete — clinical accuracy, hallucinations, harmful advice, crisis responses, privacy leaks, discriminatory outputs, prompt injection, jailbreaks, user misunderstanding.
  • Ongoing testing scheduled — post-launch and after material changes (Utah affirmative-defense policy expects this).
  • Incident workflow defined — thresholds for safety incidents, privacy incidents, licensure misrepresentation, harmful interactions, adverse outcomes.
  • User reporting mechanism live — visible in-product way to report harmful interactions, with documented review SLA.

Classification rules of thumb

Where does your product sit?

Likely lower FDA risk

General wellness education, administrative support, appointment navigation, non-patient-specific content. Avoids diagnosis, treatment, clinical action, and disease-management recommendations.

Potential Non-Device CDS

HCP-facing software meeting all four FDA criteria: no prohibited image/signal analysis, displays medical information, supports HCP recommendations, HCP can independently review the basis.

Device review likely

Consumer-facing patient-specific diagnosis, treatment, medication, triage, or disease management. Opaque recommendations an HCP cannot independently evaluate. Medical image or signal analysis. Outputs that direct clinical action without meaningful professional review.

Utah HB 452 controls apply

Generative AI engaging in therapist-like mental health conversations and represented (or reasonably perceived) as providing therapy or helping manage/treat mental health conditions for Utah users.

CHATBOT Act exposure (if enacted)

Any chatbot in health care or social assistance that falsely implies professional licensure, credentials, experience, authoritative licensed advice, or licensed human verification.

EU AI Act high-risk

Clinical AI used in the EU is generally high-risk under the AI Act. Dual-regulated with MDR/IVDR. Emotion recognition in workplaces and schools is prohibited outright under Article 5(1)(f).

Architecture recommendation

Separate general education from patient-specific clinical output.

For consumer-facing health and mental health products, the safest architecture is usually to separate general educational content from patient-specific clinical outputs. Patient-specific diagnosis, treatment, medication, triage, or crisis-management outputs should be either:

  1. Avoided entirely;
  2. Routed to a qualified HCP for prospective independent review; or
  3. Treated as a potential device-regulated function requiring FDA pathway analysis.

For mental health use cases, add a dedicated Utah HB 452 control layer for Utah users — and consider using the Utah framework as a baseline safety program even outside Utah. It operationalizes many controls that are likely to be viewed as markers of reasonable care across jurisdictions.
