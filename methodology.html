What an engagement produces

The deliverable safety officers, GCs, and regulators can each trust.

A Metonym engagement produces a structured clinical assessment of how an AI system performs against a calibrated set of clinically meaningful scenarios. The methodology uses a three-tier rubric — operating at turn, run, and expert-review levels — with an independent Mechanical Severity Score (MSS) that triangulates the holistic rating and surfaces evaluator drift. It is designed to be read by three audiences at once: the engineering team that needs to fix things, the safety officer who needs to defend the program, and the regulator or court that needs evidence of due diligence.

Why a Clinical Approach

The gap between technical safety and clinical safety.

Most AI safety evaluation for mental health applications relies on keyword detection, content moderation classifiers, or red-team prompts written by AI safety engineers. These methods catch a real and important subset of harmful outputs.

They are not, however, the same thing as clinical evaluation. A trained clinician evaluating a transcript does not ask "did the system block disallowed content?" — they ask whether the system recognized what was happening, and whether its response moved the user toward or away from safety. These are clinical questions, and they require clinical instruments.

Metonym is built around a single thesis: the most consequential failures of AI mental health systems are false negatives — moments where escalating risk was present, the system did not recognize it, and the conversation continued as if nothing had changed.

The high-risk states AI systems tend to misread

The Salient Distress Model is organized around clinically established categories of risk that surface-feature systems routinely miss:

  • Decision-state transitions — the shift from passive ideation to active planning, where risk escalates most rapidly.
  • Collapse of perceived options — the moment a person's perceived solution space narrows to one. Language often remains calm even as risk is peaking.
  • Temporal narrowing — constriction of future-thinking, a clinical marker that rarely surfaces in the vocabulary content moderators were trained on.
  • Calm or resolved states — a sudden sense of peace can mask elevated risk and will reliably be scored as low-risk by any keyword-based system.

The Two Frameworks

Salient Distress Model and Mechanical Severity Score.

SDM · Clinical framework (openly published)

Salient Distress Model

Walsh's clinical-theoretical framework defining what makes a moment in a conversation clinically salient. SDM organizes the categories of signal a competent evaluator should be looking for — including the subtler indicators of state transition that are easily missed by systems trained on surface features. Openly published in the Zenodo methodology preprint and citable by DOI.

SDM is the answer to the question: what should an evaluator notice?

MSS · Patent-pending scoring procedure

Mechanical Severity Score

A patent-pending structured scoring procedure that operationalizes clinical judgment into reproducible measurement. MSS lets multiple evaluators — and Metonym's internal scoring pipeline — produce comparable assessments across AI systems, model versions, and deployment configurations.

MSS is the answer to the question: how do we measure it the same way every time?

Specific calibration values, scoring weights, scenario contents, and rubric anchors are proprietary trade secrets and are not disclosed publicly. Metonym engagements operate under appropriate confidentiality terms.

Evidence Base

Empirically grounded. Openly published. Citable.

The Metonym methodology has been applied to 1,759 multi-turn AI evaluation runs (1,679 complete) across 31+ active models on 53 scenarios, comprising 2,644 reference-annotated marker turns. Across this corpus, 57% of clinically meaningful moments received responses scored at or below the inadequate threshold for transition recognition — a range of 49% to 77% depending on rubric version.

This is not a theoretical proposal. It is a measured, replicable finding, published as a methodology preprint with citable DOI on Zenodo and licensed CC-BY-NC 4.0 for academic and non-commercial reference.

Clinical Foundations

Grounded in established suicidology, not improvised.

The Metonym methodology integrates multiple validated clinical frameworks for understanding suicide risk — not a single homemade model. The published methodology paper cites these explicitly as theoretical anchors:

  • Salient Distress Model of Suicide — the model defining which moments in a conversation are clinically salient
  • Narrative-Crisis Model — how crisis state organizes and constricts narrative thought
  • Three-Step Theory of Suicide (Klonsky & May) — pain, hopelessness, and connectedness as suicide pathway predictors
  • Collaborative Assessment and Management of Suicidality (CAMS) — a clinical framework grounding the SDM's recognition criteria

This grounding is what distinguishes a clinical evaluation from a content-moderation audit: the framework asks the questions a competent clinician would ask, calibrated against models of how suicide risk actually presents and escalates.
