Regulation

The legal landscape for AI mental health tools solidified in 2025.

There is no single, settled duty-of-care standard yet — the law is being built in real time, mostly through litigation and state-level statutes. The picture below is a clinical view of where it stands now, with verified citations to every ruling and statute. It is not legal advice.

The precedent

Garcia v. Character Technologies — the chatbot is a product.

Megan Garcia's wrongful-death suit, filed October 2024 after her 14-year-old son Sewell Setzer III died by suicide following Character.AI use, survived motion to dismiss on May 20, 2025. Judge Anne C. Conway, in the Middle District of Florida, ruled the chatbot is a product, not protected speech for product-liability purposes.

Most of Garcia's claims — product liability, negligence, wrongful death, Florida Deceptive and Unfair Trade Practices Act, unjust enrichment — were allowed to proceed. Only the intentional infliction of emotional distress claim was dismissed. This is the ruling everyone is now planning around: it signals that First Amendment and Section 230 defenses will not reliably shield AI companion products from injury suits.

Sources: Order on Motion to Dismiss (May 2025, M.D. Fla.) · CourtListener docket · Transparency Coalition analysis

State legislation

Five state laws now define the floor.

The 2025 wave produced four operational regimes plus one in flight. Each has its own definitions, duties, and penalties. The patchwork will get worse before it consolidates.

Illinois · HB 1806

Wellness and Oversight for Psychological Resources Act

Signed by Gov. Pritzker on August 4, 2025. The strictest of the bunch. Prohibits AI from making independent therapeutic decisions, directly interacting with clients in therapeutic communication, or generating treatment plans without licensed-professional review. Administrative AI (scheduling, billing, records) is fine. Carve-outs for religious counseling, peer support, and general self-help materials.

Penalty: $10,000 civil penalty per violation.

IDFPR press release · Bill status · Holland & Knight analysis

Utah · HB 452

AI Amendments — Mental Health Chatbots

Signed March 25, 2025; effective May 7, 2025. Targets "mental health chatbots" narrowly — bots engaging in conversations similar to confidential communications with a licensed therapist (scripted bots and pure referral bots are excluded). Requires AI-status disclosure before use, after 7 days of inactivity, and on request. Requires advertising disclosures. Bans sale or sharing of individually identifiable health information.

Penalty: Up to $2,500 per violation.

Enrolled bill text (PDF) · Wilson Sonsini analysis · Future of Privacy Forum explainer

California · SB 243

Companion Chatbot Law

Signed by Gov. Newsom October 13, 2025; effective January 1, 2026. First-in-nation companion-AI safeguards, focused on minors. For known-minor users: AI-status disclosure plus break/AI reminders every 3 hours. Operators must maintain a protocol preventing the chatbot from producing suicide or self-harm content and referring at-risk users to crisis providers (988, Crisis Text Line). Reasonable measures against sexually explicit content for minors. Annual reporting to the California Department of Public Health's Office of Suicide Prevention begins July 1, 2027.

Penalty: Private right of action with damages of the greater of actual harm or $1,000 per violation.

Bill text and status · Skadden analysis · Sen. Padilla press release

New York

AI Companion Models Law

Effective November 5, 2025. Defines "AI companion" precisely: a system that simulates a sustained human relationship by retaining prior interactions, asking unprompted emotion-based questions, and sustaining dialogue about personal issues — a definition that captures Replika and Character.AI but mostly exempts general-purpose assistants. Requires AI-status disclosure plus notifications every 3 hours of continuing interaction (or daily). Requires reasonable efforts to detect and respond to suicidal ideation and self-harm, with crisis-resource referrals.

Penalty: AG enforcement; up to $15,000 per day, deposited into a state suicide prevention fund.

Governor Hochul announcement · Fenwick analysis · Manatt client alert

United States · Federal framework

No comprehensive federal AI statute — yet.

There is no national AI mental health law, and none is currently on the near horizon. Instead, AI mental health tools operate within overlapping federal frameworks that were not designed for AI but apply to it.

FDA Clinical Decision Support Software guidance

Under Section 520(o)(1)(E) of the Federal Food, Drug, and Cosmetic Act, certain software functions are excluded from the medical device definition — but the exclusion is narrow and explicitly built around support for a health care professional, not autonomous consumer-facing decision-making. The four-criteria test for Non-Device Clinical Decision Support status requires:

  1. No analysis of medical images, in vitro diagnostic signals, or signal-acquisition patterns;
  2. Display, analysis, or printing of medical information about a patient or other medical information;
  3. Recommendations directed to an HCP, not directly to a patient or caregiver;
  4. The HCP can independently review the basis for those recommendations and not rely primarily on the software output.

A patient-facing AI mental health chatbot that bypasses the HCP does not qualify for this exclusion. FDA states that existing digital health policies apply to software functions that meet the device definition, including functions intended for patients or caregivers.

Sources: FDA CDS guidance page · Full FDA CDS guidance (PDF)

Proposed CHATBOT Act (H.R. 7985)

Introduced in the 119th Congress and referred to the House Committee on Energy and Commerce. Would prohibit AI chatbots from falsely indicating or implying that the chatbot has a professional license, or that outputs are provided or verified by a licensed human professional — including in health care and social assistance professions. Enforcement would run through the FTC, state attorneys general, and a private right of action. Not yet law.

Source: H.R. 7985 bill text (govinfo) · Bill metadata

Sectoral overlay

Even absent a dedicated AI statute, the federal compliance map includes:

  • HIPAA where the tool touches protected health information;
  • FTC Act §5 — unfair and deceptive practices authority, increasingly applied to AI mental health marketing claims and false safety representations;
  • COPPA where minors are involved;
  • 21st Century Cures Act CDS carve-out for clinician-facing decision support that meets specific criteria;
  • State licensure boards — increasingly treating "AI therapist" marketing as unlicensed practice and deceptive trade practices where bots imply clinical supervision that does not exist.

European Union

EU AI Act + MDR/IVDR — dual regulation.

Most clinical AI deployed in the EU is dual-regulated: AI-enabled medical devices must comply with both the Medical Device Regulation (MDR 2017/745 or IVDR 2017/746 for diagnostics) and the EU AI Act (Regulation (EU) 2024/1689). The AI Act entered into force August 1, 2024, with obligations phasing in through 2026.

Article 5(1)(f) — emotion recognition

In force February 2, 2025. Article 5(1)(f) prohibits AI emotion recognition in workplaces and educational institutions outright. Narrow exceptions for "medical or safety purposes" — and the European Commission has interpreted "medical" to mean approved medical devices specifically, not general wellbeing inference.

High-risk classification for clinical AI

Outside the Article 5 prohibitions, most AI-enabled medical devices and clinical decision-support tools are classified as high-risk under the AI Act. High-risk status triggers requirements around data governance, technical documentation, human oversight, transparency, post-market monitoring, and conformity assessment — typically integrated with MDR/IVDR's CE-marking process. Mental-health uses can qualify under the Article 5 medical exception only if they meet device regulation standards.

Sources: EU AI Act — Article 5 (Prohibited AI Practices) · European Commission AI Act Service Desk · Future of Privacy Forum — emotion recognition prohibition

The converging duty-of-care floor

Six obligations any responsible AI mental health operator should now meet.

Distilled across these five regimes, the practical compliance floor for a nationally-deployed AI system that may encounter mental health content is now:

  • Conspicuous AI disclosure. The user knows they are talking to AI (UT, CA, NY, EU).
  • Active suicide and self-harm detection with crisis-resource referral (CA, NY).
  • Periodic break and reality-check reminders for sustained interaction (CA, NY).
  • No AI clinical decision-making without a licensed professional in the loop (IL).
  • No sale or sharing of mental-health-related identifiable data (UT).
  • No emotion inference deployed in workplaces or schools in the EU (Article 5(1)(f)).

Plus the Garcia precedent telling operators ordinary product-liability law applies — full stop.

Still in flux

What to watch into 2026–2027.

  • Garcia is at motion-to-dismiss stage only. The merits, discovery, and any settlement are still ahead. Whether the case survives appeal will determine how durable the "chatbot is a product" framing becomes.
  • More state bills are in the pipeline. Massachusetts, New Jersey, and Texas have all advanced proposals. The patchwork will get messier before it consolidates.
  • The FTC has signaled it is looking at AI mental-health claims under §5. No major enforcement action yet — but consent decrees would shift the compliance bar overnight.
  • The federal landscape is empty. There is no national AI mental-health statute and no signal one is coming. The action is at the state and judicial level.
  • Professional licensing boards are paying attention. If a real clinician's name or methods are deployed in an AI tool without their consent, board complaints become a real exposure vector.

How Metonym fits

Clinical evaluation is now the load-bearing artifact.

Every one of these regimes — implicitly or explicitly — assumes the deploying company can produce evidence that its AI was evaluated against a clinical bar, not just a technical one. Garcia makes ordinary product-liability discovery available; the state statutes layer specific duties (detection, referral, disclosure); the EU regime layers conformity assessment.
